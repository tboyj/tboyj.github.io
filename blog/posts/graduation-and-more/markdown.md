## The Intelligence Wars

For a couple months, I had turned towards AI as a replacement for thinking. And while it is very accurate in what it thinks, it is also severely inaccurate for people like me who don't do well at turning thought processes into direct wording. It never really felt rewarding, or as if I knew what I was doing was right. As to be expected, of course, but it seems so easy to grasp. So close, and yet so far. It made me realize that I am ultimately never in control unless the problem solving is spearheaded by mankind. Our mind is still more intelligent than what an AI can ever achieve, and the only difference seperating the two is quality over quantity.    

Sure, I could paste a prompt into Claude or ChatGPT or whatever AI we want to go with, I'd probably get the same results as what I could eventually achieve--but it would simply be faster. But unless you are absolutely certain that what it had generated is fine after doing code reviews on top of code reviews, there is no predeterministic way to tell if what it spat out garbage or sufficient code.

Now I'll admit... *I probably didn't use AI to it's full potential.* Like, at all. I used it like the average man who was looking for an easy way to code, to be honest.

I would repeatedly have to correct it for my lackluster prompts. But yet again, for someone like me that works better by thinking in theories, architecture and pure code, coding with pseudotext seems quite stupid in my opinion. So for that reason, I've turned back to AI-free code whilst using it as a tutor of sorts, and things have been way better. It behaves for me as a sort of second layer of searching the Internet. I don't expect it to be right anymore, I just read and figure out my own mistakes. I don't really need to automate anything, anyway, and AI was never going to be my career choice either, so I don't know why I was trying to confine myself to something that I don't truly specialize in. 

## My Beginnings in C++

Oh boy.

I really love C++ as a language, but I also hate it for how it is clearly trying to do so much. But that's why I also love it.
I won't criticize the shortcomings too much as I am still learning the ropes and I don't want to seem pretentious, unlike people who sware everything under the sword of Rust.

This one's shorter as there's less content to be said and I'm tired of writing, but basically, what I've done to incentivize learning C++ and other stuff in the future is to work towards a cognitively challenging problem that interests me instead of just being cognitively challenging. Think, for instance, mathematical formulas, algorithms, problem solving, memory management, manipulation, file parsing, stuff within the fields that I enjoy. None of the web dev stuff. That's not where my heart resides, and until I need the job, it's kind of impractical to work towards something I don't want to niche within. So, with that said, I've also gamified learning C++ for myself. I am currently learning classes for example, and instead of just reading learncpp.com and doing base level work, I instead am making a game program based on the Oregon Trail called Mercenary. It will be a band of soldiers you must control and fight for contracts against Europe and stuff. Classes include things such as people, subclasses include being a mercenary, and there's types... You get the picture. I'm applying a form of former knowledge (that being my game dev work) in order to understand C++, I guess, as it's really the only thing so far that I find actively entertains me.

I love learning C++, and these things have given me such a greater appreciation for programming and math, science and theory combined; I'm just excited for college to give me my Computer Science I course so I can start.
